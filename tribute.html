Her Story

Chien-Shiung Wu was a Chinese-born American physicist so accomplished and highly respected by her colleagues that she was nicknamed the First Lady of Physics, the Queen of Nuclear Research, and if she had to be compared to anyone, she was considered the Chinese Marie Curie.

Born in Jiangsu Province of China on May 31, 1912, Wu was raised by two progressive parents who uncommonly and strongly believed in women's education. Her parents founded the Mingde School for Girls where Wu attended elementary school. She later studied at Suzhou Girls' High, and she was so successful that she was offered a place at National Central University in Nanjing. Ultimately, she attended the University of California Berkeley where she completed her Ph.D. in 1940.

Wu was most well known for working on the Manhattan Project and for debunking a hypothetical fundamental law of physics known as the conservation of parity. Although her fellow male colleagues, Tsung-Dao Lee and Chen-Ning Yang, were awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1957 for the discovery, Wu's experiments and research went unacknowledged. The Life of Chien-Shiung Wu: A Timeline

  • 1912 - Born in Jiangsu, China
  • 1923 - Attended Suzhou Women's Normal School No. 2
  • 1930 - Attended National Central University
  • 1936 - Embarked on the SS President Hoover for the US. Arrived in San Francisco
  • 1940 - Completed her Ph.D.
  • 1942 - Married Luke Chia-Liu Yuan
  • 1944 - Joined the Manhattan Project's SAM Laboratories at Columbia University
  • 1945 - Accepted Associate Research Professor position at Columbia University
  • 1948 - Elected a fellow of the American Physical Society
  • 1954 - Became a US citizen
  • 1957 - Disproved the Conservation of Parity. Tsung-Dao Lee and Chen-Ning Yang were awarded the Nobel Prize for Physics.
  • 1958 - First woman with an honorary doctorate from Princeton University
  • 1963 - Confirmed the Conserved Vector Current Hypothesis of Richard Feynman and Murray Gell-Mann
  • 1964 - Awarded the Comstock Prize in Physics
  • 1965 - Published Beta Decay
  • 1973 - Appointed the first Michael I. Pupin Professor of Physics
  • 1975 - First female President of the American Physical Society. Awarded the National Medal of Science.
  • 1978 - First to receive Wolf Prize in Physics
  • 1990 - First living scientist to have an asteroid named after her
  • 1997 - Died in New York City, US
